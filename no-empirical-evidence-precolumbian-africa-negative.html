1. Introduction & Thesis
Thesis

Thesis: Claims of pre1 Columbian African contact with the Americas 1 including the 1Black Olmec1 hypothesis and alleged Malian transatlantic voyages 1 rest on zero empirical foundations.

No African artifacts have been recovered from controlled archaeological excavations; no African DNA appears in pre1 Columbian remains; and no primary documentary evidence confirms such contact. The consensus of archaeology, genetics, and epigraphy is unanimous: the Olmec and all other pre1 Columbian civilizations were entirely indigenous developments.

Core Argument

Empirical inquiry demands falsifiable evidence subject to peer review. Afrocentric pre1 contact claims fail this standard, relying instead on:

  • Superficial visual similarities (1they look African1).
  • Discredited 19th1 century cranial race typologies.
  • Oral or textual traditions separated from verifiable events by centuries.

2. Argument 1 1 Absence of Material Evidence
Archaeology

Empirical Standard

Archaeology relies on physical artifacts recovered through controlled excavation with documented provenance and context. If Africans had made sustained contact with Indigenous societies, we would expect securely dated artifacts with recognizable African manufacture or iconography.

Key Empirical Points

  • Zero verified artifacts: No genuine African artifact has ever been found in a controlled archaeological excavation in the New World.
  • Retracted scholarship: In 2020, The Urban Review retracted a paper by Wiggan et al. promoting 1Black Olmec1 curriculum after scholars showed it recycled long1 discredited notions based on superficial physiognomic interpretation.
  • Fraudulent / dubious objects:
    • Davenport Tablets (Iowa, 1874): allegedly Egyptian hieroglyphs in the Americas 1 later exposed as planted forgeries.
    • Tecaxic1 Calixtlahuaca head: sometimes cited as 1Roman1 or 1Old World,1 but lacks clear stratigraphic documentation and cannot bear the weight Afrocentric authors place on it.
Key line: After more than a century of intensive excavation across the Americas, the tally of verified pre1 Columbian African artifacts is exactly zero.
3. Case Study 1 The Olmec Heads
Iconography

Afrocentric Claim

Ivan Van Sertima and others argue that the colossal Olmec heads (c. 12001400 BCE) depict 1Negroid1 Africans based on broad noses and thick lips.

Empirical Refutation

Archaeologist Michael D. Coe, who excavated the major Olmec site of San Lorenzo, wrote: 1There is nothing in these Olmec sites that looks African, Chinese, European, or Near Eastern. The Olmec culture was created and maintained by American Indian peoples with a completely Mesoamerican way of life centered on the cultivation of maize.1

Forensic / Morphological Analysis

  • Olmec sculptures show traits common among modern Indigenous inhabitants of Tabasco and Veracruz:
    • Flat, broad noses
    • Thick lips
    • Occasional epicanthic folds
  • By contrast, Nubians and many West Africans often have:
    • Longer, higher noses
    • Different cranial proportions (dolichocephalic forms)
    which do not match the sculpted Olmec heads.
Key line: The 1Black Olmec1 reading is not a discovery; it is a projection of racial categories onto Indigenous faces, rejected by the very archaeologists who excavated the sites.
4. Argument 2 1 Genetic Evidence of Indigenous Origin
Ancient DNA

Why DNA Matters

Ancient DNA (aDNA) provides direct empirical evidence of ancestry. Pre1 Columbian human remains from Mesoamerica and South America have been extensively sampled. None show African haplogroups.

Key Findings

  • Indigenous haplogroups only: Pre1 Columbian remains carry mtDNA haplogroups A2, B2, C1, D1, and D4h3a 1 lineages derived from a Siberian founding population that entered the Americas ~16,000 years ago.
  • Zero African markers pre11492: No pre1 Columbian remains exhibit African mtDNA haplogroups (L11L3) or African Y1 DNA lineages (E1b1a, R1 V88, etc.). African genetic signatures appear only after 1492, in contexts associated with European colonial populations.
  • Large1 scale peer1 reviewed work: A study of 229 unadmixed Native American individuals across Mesoamerica and South America identified seven distinct Indigenous genetic components with 1no detectable European admixture1 in ancient samples 1 and, by the same logic, no African admixture.
Key line: The genomes of pre1 Columbian peoples across the Americas are entirely Indigenous. Africa1s genetic signature only appears in the Americas after the slave trade begins.
5. Case Study 1 Teotihuacan & Maya aDNA
Site Evidence

Teotihuacan (Classic Period)

Analysis of 36 individuals from multi1 ethnic neighborhoods at Teotihuacan found only Indigenous haplogroups:

  • A: 58%
  • B: 25%
  • C: 14%
  • D: 3%

This pattern matches known Indigenous Mesoamerican diversity and shows no African contribution.

Midnight Terror Cave, Belize (5501900 CE)

17 mitogenomes from sacrificial victims yielded:

  • A2: 82%
  • B2: 12%
  • C1: 6%

Again, no African lineages.

As summarized in Current Anthropology (1997), all ancient mitochondrial lineages detected in these studies are absent from African data sets 1 they are Indigenous, not African.

Key line: At the level of specific sites and burials, from Teotihuacan neighborhoods to Maya sacrificial caves, the genetic picture is the same: Indigenous only, with no African ancestry signal.
6. Argument 3 1 Documentary Evidence Is Weak or Absent

Malian Voyages (1311 CE)

  • Single1 source dependency: The story of a Malian Atlantic fleet derives solely from al1 Umari (c. 1337), who heard it from Mansa Musa in Cairo. It is a late, second1 hand account with no corroborating records.
  • Name confusion: The often1 cited 1Abu Bakr II1 is a 19th1 century mistranslation by Baron de Slane; the original Arabic text does not give this name, only identifying Musa1s predecessor.
  • No empirical footprint: The fleet 1never returned1; there are no known shipwrecks, coastal settlements, or African artifacts in Caribbean or Mesoamerican strata dated to this period.

Columbus, Las Casas, and Guan1n

Van Sertima also cites Bartolom1 de las Casas1s abstract of Columbus1s log, recording Taino claims that 1from the south and the southeast had come black people whose spears were made of a metal called guan1n.1

  • Local metallurgy: Guan1n is a Taino term for a gold1 silver1 copper alloy whose composition matches local Caribbean metallurgy, not West African sources.
  • Unconfirmed rumor: Columbus1s interest was in testing Portuguese rumors of 1canoes from Guinea1; he never confirmed direct African contact.
  • Likely Indigenous referent: The 1black people1 in question were almost certainly darker1 skinned Indigenous Caribs, not Africans 1 and, again, no archaeological trace links them to Africa.
Key line: A couple of ambiguous stories, filtered through centuries of copying, with zero physical or genetic corroboration, cannot overturn the entire archaeological and genetic record of the Americas.
