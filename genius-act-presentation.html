PART I / CBDC BAN
PART 01  /  THE SETUP

The Government Said No
to a Digital Dollar

The headline was clean: The U.S. government banned development of a Central Bank Digital Currency. No CBDC. Full stop. The official reasoning leaned into civil liberties — privacy concerns, fear of government surveillance, too much centralized control over money.

Politically, that message landed. "We don't want the government tracking every transaction." Sounds like freedom. Sounds principled.

The Twist At the exact same time the CBDC ban was signed… the government explicitly endorsed private, dollar-backed stablecoins as the future of digital money.

So instead of:
  Government → issuing digital dollars

We now have:
  Private companies → issuing digital dollars
  Backed by… the government.

That's not removing control. That's outsourcing it.

The ban on a CBDC didn't kill the concept of a digital dollar. It just changed whose name was on the door. The infrastructure, the backing, the government benefit — all of that remained. What changed was the entity capturing the upside.

PART II / THE ACT
PART 02  /  THE FRAMEWORK

Enter the GENIUS Act

In 2025, Congress passed the GENIUS Act — the first real federal framework for stablecoins. It wasn't crypto regulation in the traditional sense. It was closer to a charter for a new class of financial institution.

1
Rule One
Full 1:1 Backing Required — no fractional reserves. Every digital dollar must equal a real dollar or government debt.
↓
2
Rule Two
Reserves must be held in cash, bank deposits, or short-term U.S. Treasuries. Treasury-backed by default.
↓
3
Rule Three — The Key One
Stablecoin issuers are banned from paying interest to users. The yield from reserves? Stays with the issuer.
Translation When you hold a stablecoin, you are indirectly holding U.S. Treasuries — and indirectly funding the U.S. government. But you receive none of the yield those Treasuries generate. That yield belongs to the company that issued your "money."

PART IV / DESIGN
PART 04  /  THIS ISN'T ACCIDENTAL

Three Problems.
One Elegant Structure.

The GENIUS Act framework isn't a coincidence. It simultaneously solves three major problems for the financial and political establishment.

Problem 1 — Bank Run Risk Lawmakers were worried: if stablecoins paid interest, people would pull deposits from traditional banks in droves. Solution? Remove yield entirely. Banks remain competitive. Systemic risk neutralized.
Problem 2 — Dollar Dominance Stablecoins are globally accessible, dollar-denominated, and Treasury-backed. The U.S. now exports digital dollars worldwide — without printing physical cash. The dollar's reserve currency status is reinforced in the digital era without the Fed lifting a finger.
Problem 3 — Cheaper Government Debt The sleeper effect. Stablecoin issuers become major, permanent buyers of U.S. Treasuries. More demand = lower yields = lower borrowing costs for the government. Research indicates this demand can meaningfully reduce Treasury yields — saving the government billions annually.
Your money works. Just not for you.

Functionally? These systems are extremely similar. The principal difference — the one distinction that matters most in terms of real-world outcomes — is who captures the yield generated by your deposits.

Under a CBDC: the government controls it, government benefits. Under GENIUS stablecoins: private sector profits, government still benefits indirectly. You, in both scenarios, hold a digital dollar that earns you nothing.
